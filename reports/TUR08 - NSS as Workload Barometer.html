TUR08 — NSS as Workload Barometer

National Student Survey themes 3 & 4 (Assessment and feedback; Academic support) vs the staff-student ratio, 2023–2026. University of East London (UKPRN 10007144).

TRADE UNION REPORT SERIES · TUR08
HONEST RESULT This report set out to test whether NSS satisfaction on staff-facing themes tracks the rising staff-student ratio. Over the only four years NSS data covers (2023–2026), it doesn't: every NSS theme score, including Assessment and feedback and Academic support, has risen every year – the opposite of what a simple "more students per staff member = lower satisfaction" hypothesis would predict. This is reported as found; it does not support the workload hypothesis in this window, and the limitations below explain why that's not necessarily the end of the story.

Reading these numbers

WHY THIS ISN'T A CLEAN TEST Three real limits mean this comparison can't settle the workload question either way: (1) NSS only covers 2023–2026 – four data points, two of which (2023/24, 2024/25) fall in the exact years the staff-student ratio itself is flagged as data-quality-affected. (2) NSS asks graduating final-year students about their whole course experience, which reflects staffing and workload conditions from several years earlier, not the same-year ratio – a genuine lag this simple year-on-year comparison can't capture. (3) Response rates rose over the same period (75.5% → 79.6%), which can itself shift which students' views are captured.
What this does show: UEL's NSS scores on every theme, including the two most workload-sensitive ones, have been on a genuinely strong upward trajectory (Assessment and feedback: 84.7% → 90.8%; Academic support: 82.3% → 89.7%), moving from below benchmark in 2023 (Academic support 82.3% vs 83.5% benchmark) to at or above benchmark by 2026 (89.7% vs 89.5%). Whatever pressure the rising staff-student ratio has put on staff, it has not (yet, or not visibly) shown up as falling student satisfaction on these measures.
A fairer test of the workload hypothesis would need a longer NSS run matched to the years when the ratio was rising fastest (2018/19–2021/22, per TUR07) – NSS scores for those exact years are listed as "still outstanding" in Data/DataIndex.md. If those are ever obtained, this report should be rebuilt with the fuller series before drawing a firmer conclusion.
