TRADE UNION REPORT SERIES · TUR01
HEADLINE The share of UEL's academic staff on fixed-term contracts rose from 31.5% in 2014/15 to a peak of 46.3% in 2023/24 (currently 42.7% in 2024/25) — while the England-wide sector average moved in the opposite direction, falling from 36.8% to 28.0% over the same period. UEL started this period slightly below the national casualisation rate and now sits well above it.

READ WITH CAUTION The right-hand chart is the England sector average, not a UEL figure – HESA Staff Table 7 does not publish UEL's contract type cross-tabulated by teaching/research function. Nationally, research-only staff are by far the most casualised group (64.7% fixed-term), versus just 5.9% for staff who are both teaching and research (typically standard permanent lecturer posts). If UEL follows the national pattern, its research-only staff (40 people in 2024/25) are disproportionately exposed – but this cannot be confirmed from open data at UEL level.

Reading these numbers

The core bargaining fact: UEL's fixed-term share of academic staff grew by roughly 11 percentage points (31.5% → 42.7%, having peaked at 46.3% the year before) over a decade in which the England sector average fell by almost 9 points (36.8% → 28.0%). This is not a story of "the whole sector is casualising" – on this metric, UEL has moved sharply against the national trend.
Casualisation is not evenly spread: part-time roles have been majority fixed-term throughout the period (53.8%–73.8%), while full-time fixed-term contracts, though a much smaller share (4.5%–12.5%), have nearly tripled in relative terms. The growth in "Other contract level" headcount (800 → 1,000, +25%) against flat Professor/Senior academic numbers (110 → 95) is consistent with this growth sitting in more junior, less secure posts.
This report does not include hourly-paid and specialist lecturers, who HESA Staff Table 7 does not capture on a headcount basis at all (they are effectively invisible to "Terms of employment" here). UEL's own 2024/25 Annual Report discloses 641 hourly-paid/specialist lecturers paid £3.46m in 2024/25 (2024: 711 people, £4.96m) – the University's own illustrative estimate is that this is equivalent to roughly 39 FTE academic posts (2024: 62) if an average lecturer cost were applied. That population sits entirely outside the figures in this report and would make the true casualisation picture look worse, not better, if it could be added in.
