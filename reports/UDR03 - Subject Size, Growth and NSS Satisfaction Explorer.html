Growth vs. NSS change – the lag-aligned test

Each point plots a subject's HESA enrolment growth (year-on-year) against the NSS score change for the matching academic year (HESA 2023/24 aligns with NSS 2024, since NSS surveys final-year students during that academic year). This is the closest thing to a direct test of "does faster growth coincide with bigger NSS swings?" that these two datasets allow.
KEY LIMITATION The biggest NSS jump in the whole dataset is 2025→2026 – and there is no HESA enrolment release yet for the matching academic year (2025/26), since HESA data lags about a year behind. So the single most interesting NSS movement cannot currently be tested against enrolment growth at all. Everything below the 2025→2026 change is inference from the years that do overlap, not a direct read of the year that matters most.

What the numbers actually show

Computing roughly doubled in size (1,965 → 4,645 enrolments, +136% over four years) and Business and management grew 71% (5,305 → 9,075). Together they went from 37% of UEL's enrolment in 2021/22 to 52% in 2024/25 – a real, substantial concentration shift, not a rounding artefact.
DOESN'T FIT THE SIMPLE STORY If rapid growth alone explained rising NSS scores, Computing's NSS positivity should have tracked its enrolment upward through 2022–2024. It didn't: Computing's Teaching/Registering positivity was essentially flat-to-declining across that period (90.2% in 2023 → 87.7% in both 2024 and 2025) even as enrolment surged. Its NSS score only jumped in 2026 (91.9–92.0%) – the one year we have no matching enrolment figure for. So the data we can check doesn't support "growth drove the improvement" for Computing; the year it might support is exactly the year we can't check yet.
Business and management is more consistent with your thesis: its biggest single-year enrolment jump (7,305→9,075, +24%) landed in 2024/25, the same year its Teaching NSS score jumped most (89.5%→94.1%, matching NSS 2025). But Registering fell in that same window (89.4%→82.8% the year before, only partially recovering), so even this one is a mixed, not clean, signal.
Meanwhile Psychology, Education and teaching, and Social sciences all shrank 25–34% over the same four years – and Psychology and Education are two of the subjects flagged elsewhere as NSS underperformers. That's the opposite pairing to your thesis (shrinking, not growing, subjects showing weaker scores), which is worth holding alongside the Computing/Business evidence rather than instead of it.

Bottom line: this is genuine, useful context, and the size argument (Business + Computing = 52% of UEL, so their trends dominate institutional averages almost mechanically) stands regardless. But the direct "growth causes NSS improvement" test is inconclusive with the years available, and the year that matters most for it isn't in the HESA data yet.
