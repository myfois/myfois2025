Trends2025→2026, and the four-year picture

Every theme improved in 2026 – but by very different amounts

All 7 themes rose year-on-year for both Registering and Teaching students. The size of the improvement, though, varies a lot by theme – and the pattern is consistent across both cohorts, which is a good sign it reflects something real rather than sampling noise.

Read: Organisation & management and Student voice were UEL's two weakest themes as recently as 2023–2025 (Org&Man sat in the mid-to-high 70s). Both have closed much of the gap to benchmark in 2026, which is the single biggest structural change in this data. Teaching on my course was already strong and improved only modestly – there's less headroom left there.

The four-year shape

This isn't a one-year blip. Both cohorts have risen every single year since 2023, and the improvement has accelerated: the 2025→2026 rise (+3.2 to +3.5ppt on average across themes) is larger than either of the two prior year-on-year moves. Response rates have moved the same way (see Turnout), which supports treating the improvement as genuine rather than an artefact of who chose to respond.

AnomaliesThings that stand out and are worth asking "why"

Teaching vs. Registering gaps by subject, 2026

Most subjects score almost identically whether you look at students registered at UEL or students taught at UEL – expected, since the two cohorts mostly overlap. Three subjects break that pattern noticeably:

SubjectTeachingRegisteringGap (Teaching − Registering)
Media, journalism and communications82.8%78.4%+4.4
Business and management94.3%90.9%+3.4
Biological and sport sciences89.1%86.8%+2.3
In every one of these, Teaching students are notably happier than Registering students in the same subject area. Since Teaching = students taught at UEL regardless of where they're registered, and Registering = students registered at UEL regardless of where they're taught, a gap this size usually points to a franchise/partner arrangement: students registered at UEL but taught elsewhere (or vice versa) are pulling the Registering-cohort average down relative to the students UEL itself is directly teaching. Worth checking against partner/franchise numbers for these three subjects specifically.

A genuine, consistent decline: Architecture

Architecture, building and planning fell −4.7ppt in both Teaching and Registering cohorts between 2025 and 2026 – the only subject area to move backwards at all, and it did so identically in both splits. Because both cohorts moved together and by the same amount, this looks like a real, subject-specific decline rather than noise or a cohort-mix effect, even though the underlying response count (36–41) is moderate rather than large.

A swing to treat with caution: Physical sciences

Physical sciences posted the largest year-on-year improvement of any subject (+10.4ppt in both cohorts) and is now UEL's top-ranked subject area. It's also, by a wide margin, UEL's smallest subject by response count (13 responses in both 2025 and 2026). A double-digit swing on a base of 13 people is well within the range you'd expect from ordinary variation, not necessarily a sign that something changed. Encouraging, but not something to build a headline claim on without more data.

Where UEL clearly beats benchmark

Computing and Business and management are UEL's most consistent over-performers: both are flagged materially above benchmark (OfS statistical test, >95% probability) on nearly every one of the 7 themes, in both Registering and Teaching. This is the mirror image of the Psychology/Media pattern above, and with much larger, more reliable response bases behind it.

FLAG Apprenticeship students score roughly 8–9 percentage points below Full-time students, identically in both Registering and Teaching splits, on a response base (113) too large to dismiss as noise. This is a clear, consistent, and moderately well-evidenced gap – arguably the most actionable finding in this whole report, since it's a defined student population UEL can look at directly (apprenticeship programme support, employer liaison, off-the-job study time) rather than a single subject or course.

By level of study, First degree and Other undergraduate students track closely for Registering (88.1% vs 89.5%), but for Teaching students, Other undergraduate is noticeably lower than First degree (86.9% vs 89.4%) – though the Other undergraduate response base here is small (around 35–47), so this is a pattern to watch rather than a settled conclusion.

Data notesMethodology and caveats

Source: Office for Students NSS provider data for UEL (UKPRN 10007144), 2023–2026, Registering and Teaching tabs. All figures above use "All modes / All undergraduates" as the base unless a mode/level split is explicitly discussed.

No single "overall satisfaction" score exists for English providers since the 2023 redesign; all analysis here is built from the 7 core NSS themes. "Materially above/below benchmark" labels follow the OfS's own statistical classification (a >50% probability threshold on the difference-from-benchmark distribution), not just a raw comparison of two numbers. Small-sample subjects and courses (response counts under roughly 20–25) are flagged explicitly wherever they appear, since NSS's own publication threshold is only 10 responses and a 50% response rate – low enough that individual results can be volatile.

This report was generated from the same underlying dataset as the companion interactive dashboard ("UEL NSS Explorer.html"), which lets you explore any subject, course, mode, level, year combination, and benchmark toggle directly.
